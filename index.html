Where Do Evacuees Go? Understanding Evacuation Destinations in Georgia during Hurricane Milton

Authors : Chaeyeon Han, Subhrajit Guhathakurta
School of City and Regional Planning, Georgia Institute of Technology, Atlanta, GA, USA

üí° Overview

Hurricane Milton triggered large-scale population movements from Florida into neighboring states, with Georgia emerging as one of the most important receiving regions. This project investigates where evacuees traveled during the event and why certain places attracted more visitors than others.

Using high-resolution mobility data and county-level social connection measures, we analyze both spatial patterns and behavioral mechanisms shaping evacuation destination choices. Two questions guide the analysis:

  • R1: Which locations in Georgia experienced notable increases in visitor activity during Hurricane Milton?
  • R2: How did evacuation orders and social ties influence evacuees‚Äô destination choices?

The findings provide new insights into how infrastructure, amenities, and social networks interact to direct climate-induced population movements.

To capture differences in behavior, we distinguish Florida origin tracts that were under mandatory evacuation orders from those without such orders. Aggregating visitor share changes by these categories reveals distinct evacuation destinations across Georgia.

Tracts in Georgia that show high visitor share increases are more strongly connected to origins with mandatory orders (hence darker shade areas in the left map). In contrast, evacuees from non-mandatory areas tend to be distributed more broadly across the state.

üó∫Ô∏è Data & Study Area

We draw on anonymized, device-based mobility traces aggregated to the census-tract level to estimate weekly visitation flows from Florida to Georgia. Following the practice from existing studies (Anyidoho et al., 2023; Hong & Frias-Martinez, 2020), baseline mobility conditions are captured by weekday-specific average visit counts three weeks prior to the hurricane period, which serve as a prediction of baseline activity. Deviations from these predicted values reveal anomalous increases associated with Hurricane Milton.

Florida Division of Emergency Management (FDEM) organizes evacuation zones in Florida that differ in exposure to storm surge and wind hazards. Hurricane Milton‚Äôs track (NOAA's HURDAT2 Dataset) intersect several of these zones. Based on news articles and county government agencies' announcements, we identified counties that issued mandatory evacuation orders for evacuation zones. These areas become the primary origins of evacuees traveling toward Georgia.

To quantify how destinations in Georgia were affected, we computed visitor share change for each census tract. This metric measures how much a tract‚Äôs share of visitors from Florida increased during the hurricane week relative to its typical share under normal conditions. If a tract normally receives n% of visitors but receives (n + d) % during the storm, the increase d captures the additional demand placed on that area.

As shown in the histogram below, most tracts experienced modest changes, but a small subset saw disproportionately large increases. This heavy right tail indicates that evacuees did not disperse evenly.

üõ†Ô∏è Quantile Regression Analysis

As seen in Figure 4, the distribution of visitor share change is highly skewed. However, a standard linear regression focuses on the mean and would mostly describe what happens in ‚Äútypical‚Äù tracts, potentially missing the dynamics that matter most for evacuation response, the places that become hotspots.

Quantile regression is designed for this type of problem. Instead of estimating how predictors affect the average outcome, it estimates their effects at different points of the distribution (for example, the 10th, 50th, or 90th percentile of visitor share change). This allows us to see how the role of social ties, accessibility, or amenities changes between lightly affected areas and those experiencing extreme increases in evacuee arrivals.

This approach is well suited to evacuation data for several reasons:

  • It captures heterogeneous effects, showing that some factors matter mainly for high-visitor tracts rather than everywhere.(Rodriguez & Yao, 2017)
  • It is more robust to outliers and heavy tails than mean-based models, which is important when a few tracts receive a disproportionate number of evacuees. (Liao & Wang, 2012)
  • It does not require constant variance of errors, making it appropriate when variability grows with the level of visitor change.

Interpretation follows the same logic: a positive coefficient for a variable at the 0.9 quantile means that, holding other variables constant, an increase in that variable raises the 90th percentile of visitor share change. If the same coefficient is close to zero at the median, we learn that the factor is especially important for high-impact destinations rather than for the average tract. This perspective helps identify which characteristics are most strongly associated with becoming an evacuation hotspot.

üîç Determinants of Visitor Concentration

The quantile regression results provide a detailed picture of what drives evacuees toward particular destinations in Georgia.

Social ties play a central role: tracts with stronger social connections to Florida origins attract significantly more evacuees, particularly at upper quantiles. This highlights how personal networks guide destination choice when uncertainty is high.

Distance and accessibility measures also matter. Greater distance from the storm‚Äôs trajectory is associated with slightly higher visitor shares, reflecting risk avoidance. Proximity to POI centers and to the Atlanta airport has strong positive effects in higher quantiles.

Finally, amenities and local context shape concentration. Hospitality infrastructure, specifically hotels and malls, is positively associated with visitor surges, while gas stations show weaker or even negative relationships at the top of the distribution. Higher-income destination tracts also tend to experience larger increases among the most-visited locations.

Discussion & Conclusion

Taken together, the results show that evacuation destinations are not chosen randomly or evenly across space. Instead, evacuees visited to a relatively small set of tracts that combine strong social ties to Florida, good regional accessibility, and available hospitality POIs. These places could effectively function as informal hubs in the evacuation network.

From a planning perspective, this means that managing hurricane evacuations is not only about moving people out of high-risk areas, but also about preparing the locations that are most likely to receive them. Enhancing shelter capacity, fuel availability, and logistical support in socially connected, amenity-rich, and well-connected destinations can reduce congestion and strain on local systems during future events.

Moreover, future work could examine how resource availability, housing markets, and information access affect who is able to reach safer, better-served destinations and who is left with fewer options. Furthermore, in the receiving places' perspective, researchers should identify evacuation hotspots and predict the impact of population influx on resource demands and economic stability.

Finally, the study illustrates the value of combining large-scale mobility data with social network measures and distribution-sensitive models such as quantile regression. This framework can be extended to other hazards, receiving regions, and time periods to better understand how human mobility contributes to regional resilience in a changing climate.
