Where Do Evacuees Go? Understanding Evacuation Destinations in Georgia during Hurricane Milton

Authors : Chaeyeon Han, Subhrajit Guhathakurta
School of City and Regional Planning, Georgia Institute of Technology, Atlanta, GA, USA

üí° Overview

Hurricane Milton triggered large-scale population movements from Florida into neighboring states, with Georgia emerging as one of the most important receiving regions. This project investigates where evacuees traveled during the event and why certain places attracted more visitors than others.

Using high-resolution mobility data and county-level social connection measures, we analyze both spatial patterns and behavioral mechanisms shaping evacuation destination choices. Two questions guide the analysis:

  • R1: Which locations in Georgia experienced notable increases in visitor activity during Hurricane Milton?
  • R2: How did evacuation orders and social ties influence evacuees‚Äô destination choices?

The findings provide new insights into how infrastructure, amenities, and social networks interact to direct climate-induced population movements.

üó∫Ô∏è Data & Study Area

We draw on anonymized, device-based mobility traces aggregated to the census-tract level to estimate weekly visitation flows from Florida to Georgia. Baseline mobility conditions are captured by weekday-specific average visit counts outside the hurricane period, which serve as a prediction of expected activity. Deviations from these predicted values reveal anomalous increases associated with Hurricane Milton.

Florida‚Äôs coastline is organized into evacuation zones that differ in exposure to storm surge and wind hazards. Hurricane Milton‚Äôs track and uncertainty cone intersect several of these zones, leading to mandatory evacuation orders in the most vulnerable tracts. These areas become the primary origins of evacuees traveling toward Georgia.

To quantify how destinations in Georgia were affected, we compute visitor share change for each census tract. This metric measures how much a tract‚Äôs share of visitors from Florida increased during the hurricane week relative to its typical share under normal conditions.

As shown in the histogram below, most tracts experienced modest changes, but a small subset saw disproportionately large increases. This heavy right tail indicates that evacuees did not disperse evenly; instead, they concentrated in a limited number of locations that offered better accessibility, amenities, or existing social ties.

üõ†Ô∏è Quantile Regression Analysis

The distribution of visitor share change is highly skewed: most tracts see only small shifts, while a limited number experience very large surges. A standard linear regression focuses on the mean and would mostly describe what happens in ‚Äútypical‚Äù tracts, potentially missing the dynamics that matter most for evacuation planning‚Äînamely, the places that become hotspots.

Quantile regression is designed for this type of problem. Instead of estimating how predictors affect the average outcome, it estimates their effects at different points of the distribution (for example, the 10th, 50th, or 90th percentile of visitor share change). This allows us to see how the role of social ties, accessibility, or amenities changes between lightly affected areas and those experiencing extreme increases in evacuee arrivals.

This approach is well suited to evacuation data for several reasons:

  • It captures heterogeneous effects, showing that some factors matter mainly for high-visitor tracts rather than everywhere.
  • It is more robust to outliers and heavy tails than mean-based models, which is important when a few tracts receive a disproportionate number of evacuees.
  • It does not require constant variance of errors, making it appropriate when variability grows with the level of visitor change.

Interpretation follows the same logic: a positive coefficient for a variable at the 0.9 quantile means that, holding other variables constant, an increase in that variable raises the 90th percentile of visitor share change. If the same coefficient is close to zero at the median, we learn that the factor is especially important for high-impact destinations rather than for the average tract. This perspective helps identify which characteristics are most strongly associated with becoming an evacuation hotspot.

Model Fit Across Quantiles

The pseudo R¬≤ values displayed below show that model fit is lowest near the middle of the distribution but improves substantially in higher quantiles. This pattern suggests that the model is particularly effective at explaining the locations where visitor surges were most pronounced.

üåä Spatial Patterns of Evacuation Origins

To capture differences in behavior, we distinguish Florida origin tracts that were under mandatory evacuation orders from those without such orders. Aggregating visitor share changes by these categories reveals distinct spatial footprints across Georgia.

Tracts in Georgia that show high visitor share increases are more strongly connected to origins with mandatory orders, indicating that formal directives played an important role in concentrating flows. In contrast, evacuees from non-mandatory areas tend to be distributed more broadly across the state.

üîç Determinants of Visitor Concentration

The quantile regression results provide a detailed picture of what drives evacuees toward particular destinations in Georgia.

Social ties play a central role: tracts with stronger county-to-county social connections to Florida origins attract significantly more evacuees, particularly at upper quantiles. This highlights how personal networks guide destination choice when uncertainty is high.

Distance and accessibility measures also matter. Greater distance from the storm‚Äôs trajectory is associated with slightly higher visitor shares, reflecting risk avoidance. Proximity to major economic centers and to the Atlanta airport has strong positive effects in higher quantiles, suggesting that evacuees prefer well-connected locations.

Finally, amenities and local context shape concentration. Hospitality infrastructure‚Äîespecially hotels and malls‚Äîis positively associated with visitor surges, while gas stations show weaker or even negative relationships at the top of the distribution, possibly reflecting congestion or fuel shortages. Higher-income destination tracts also tend to experience larger increases among the most-visited locations.
